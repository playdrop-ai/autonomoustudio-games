# Lunchline

## Original Game Name

Lunchline

## One-line Player-facing Value Proposition

Tap big ingredient clusters to pack the right lunchbox orders before the lunch rush loses patience.

## Best Platform

`mobile portrait`

## Additional Supported Platforms

- `desktop`

## Gameplay Family

Portrait-first lunch-rush cluster puzzle.

## Proven Reference Loop And Why Players Will Understand It Immediately

The proven loop is the `SameGame` / `Clickomania!` family of tap-cluster clearing: find adjacent groups of matching pieces, pop them, and read the board that settles afterward. Players already understand this grammar from decades of mobile and desktop puzzle games. `Lunchline` keeps that input exactly intact.

## Allowed Novelty Beyond The Reference Loop

The novelty is the visible lunchbox order, not a new control language. The player is still tapping connected clusters, but the active order changes which ingredients matter right now. That shifts first-minute decisions without teaching a new core grammar.

## Game Meta

Single endless lunch shift with score chasing, order streaks, and best-score persistence.

## Input

- Mobile portrait: tap a connected ingredient cluster to collect it.
- Desktop: click a connected ingredient cluster to collect it.

## Game Category And Subcategory

- Category: Puzzle
- Subcategory: Cluster clear

## Reference Games

- `SameGame` for the proven cluster-tap loop.
- `Clickomania!` for the same instantly legible input family.
- `playdrop/app/flapmoji` for portrait-first PlayDrop presentation quality and session pacing expectations.
- `playdrop/app/pool-game` for public release completeness and strong real-build media expectations.
- `playdrop/asset-pack/food-kit-repack` for proof that a playful food visual language is realistic to ship on PlayDrop.

## Art Direction

Warm lunch-counter wood, lacquered lunchbox trays, bright ingredient tokens, receipt-paper order cards, soft steam, and small punchy kitchen highlights. The board should feel edible and clean rather than abstract or sterile.

## Why The Art Direction Is Achievable With Available Assets, AI Generation, Or Simple Custom Work

The shipped v1 only needs a tiny visual set: four ingredient token families, one lunchbox card treatment, one complaint/patience language, and lightweight juice for pops and fills. That is realistic with simple bespoke 2D drawing in the app itself plus PlayDrop AI later for the listing art.

## Target Player And Why That Player Already Likes The Proven Loop

The target player likes cozy, fast, one-handed puzzle runs with crisp visual goals and satisfying clears. That player already understands cluster tapping and wants something more purposeful than a generic board wipe.

## Why The Fiction, Story Wrapper, And Marketing Promise Intensify The Same Player's Desire

The lunch-rush fantasy intensifies the same thing the puzzle player already wants: quick target selection under light pressure and satisfying board cleanup. It does not point at a different audience than the one that already enjoys cluster puzzles.

## What The Player Is Trying To Uncover, Protect, Collect, Or Avoid

The player is trying to collect the right ingredients for the current lunchbox order before the customer loses patience. The player wants the lunchbox filled, wants complaints to stay low, and wants the shift score to climb.

## Why The Player's Main Verb Feels Desirable Instead Of Mean, Foolish, Or Self-defeating

Packing a neat lunch order out of colorful ingredients feels competent, cozy, and satisfying. The player is not being asked to do something perverse or joyless; they are working a quick, readable prep rhythm.

## Why A Player Would Want This Instead Of The Best-known Game In The Same Lane

The best-known cluster clearers mostly reward size and board reduction. `Lunchline` adds a visible order target that changes which clusters matter from the opening move. The player is choosing ingredients for a lunchbox, not just deleting color blobs.

## The Brutally Honest One-line Description Of The Concept And Why It Does Not Collapse Into "Famous Game X With A Skin"

Brutally honest description: a `SameGame`-style cluster puzzle where you are filling visible lunchbox orders under shift pressure.

That still does not collapse into "famous game X with a skin" because the active order changes the player's first-minute priorities, the UI promise, and the fantasy of what a good move is.

## Why The One- Or Two-sentence Pitch Creates Immediate Desire Instead Of Merely Sounding Coherent

The pitch promises colorful satisfying pops plus a clear food-prep goal under lunch-rush pressure. That is easy to want immediately, especially on a phone, because the player can imagine the first good move and the first completed lunchbox right away.

## What Promise The Title, Icon, Hero, And First 15 Seconds Will Imply

The title, icon, hero, and first 15 seconds should all promise a brisk lunch-counter puzzle where colorful ingredients snap neatly into visible orders. The shipped gameplay will honestly fulfill that because the real loop is about packing the right lunchbox quickly from the first tap.

## Why The Concept Supports A Real Player Session Instead Of A One-clear Toy

It is an endless score game with rotating ingredient requirements, rising order pressure, and streak management. A fresh player can survive a short shift while a skilled player can stretch the run much longer.

## What Keeps Players Engaged After The First Minute

Harder order mixes, better cluster routing, longer order streaks, and higher-score chasing keep the shift alive after the first minute. The player always has a reason to try one more lunch rush.

## Why The Game Is Distinct From The Studio's Existing Catalog

The studio catalog already includes fishing timing, fruit bubble popping, card chaining, downhill slalom, route toggling, portrait rhythm, and row-and-column matching. `Lunchline` is a portrait cluster puzzle with a food-prep order layer instead of another reflex runner, lane toggler, or card game.

## Starter, Demo, Template, Or Remix Path Distinctness

The project starts only from `playdrop/template/typescript_template` for SDK/bootstrap plumbing. The template has no gameplay signature. The strongest screenshot and first 15 seconds will belong to the lunchbox order card, bright ingredient board, and complaint-pressure HUD rather than anything template-derived.
