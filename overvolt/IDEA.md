# Overvolt

## Original Game Name

Overvolt

## One-line Player-facing Value Proposition

Skid an RC battle car around a tabletop arena, slam rival toys into the rails, and steal their dropped batteries before your own charge runs dry.

## Best Platform

`mobile landscape`

## Additional Supported Platforms

- `desktop`

## Gameplay Family

Landscape direct-control vehicle survival brawler.

## Proven Reference Loop And Why Players Will Understand It Immediately

The core loop is a proven battle-car survival lane: move continuously, line up rams, avoid incoming rivals, and stay alive longer than the wave pressure. Players already understand direct steering plus collision-based knockback from arcade driving and arena-brawler games. `Overvolt` keeps that grammar intact: steer, dash, ram, collect batteries, survive.

## Allowed Novelty Beyond The Reference Loop

The novelty stays outside the primary interaction grammar:

- battery is both health and score momentum, so aggression feeds survival
- the fantasy is a bright tabletop RC arena instead of a track, warzone, or open vehicle sandbox
- there are no guns, laps, or routing systems; winning comes from positioning, bump angles, and charge timing

The player still learns one main action language: steer the car, burst with dash, hit rivals harder than they hit you, and collect charge.

## Game Meta

One endless run, escalating wave pressure, local best-score persistence, and no account progression in v1.

## Input

- Mobile landscape: left-thumb virtual stick to steer, right-thumb `Dash` button for the short electric burst.
- Desktop: `WASD` or arrow keys to steer, `Space` for dash.

## Game Category And Subcategory

- Category: Arcade
- Subcategory: Vehicle combat survival

## Reference Games

- `Death Rally`-style arena car combat for the direct read of ramming and surviving.
- `Smash Karts` for the broad appetite for toy-like car combat, while `Overvolt` deliberately removes weapons and lap-race structure.
- Real-world RC tabletop tracks and bumper-car fantasy for the bright tactile toy appeal.

## Art Direction

Bright tabletop toy garage: warm wood desk surface, white tape guard rails, chunky plastic RC cars, blue battery shards, orange spark bursts, and a few simple desk props like cones, boxes, binder clips, and pencils. The camera is top-down with a small tilt so the cars feel physical without hiding the arena read.

## Why The Art Direction Is Achievable

The game can ship with a very small asset set:

- a handful of local Kenney toy-car or car-kit GLBs already mirrored on disk
- simple primitive arena pieces for the tabletop, rails, and props
- one clean HUD layer in DOM
- a small FX pass of sparks, skid marks, and battery glow

It does not need characters, animation rigs, or content-heavy level production.

## Target Player And Why They Already Like The Proven Loop

The target player is someone who likes quick arcade control, short aggressive runs, and readable physical chaos on mobile landscape or desktop. They already like direct steering, knockback, and one-more-run score pressure.

## Why The Fiction, Story Wrapper, And Marketing Promise Intensify The Same Player's Desire

The tabletop RC fantasy strengthens the same direct-control appeal instead of aiming at a different audience. The promise is still quick, tactile, aggressive play; the toy wrapper makes it lighter, clearer, and more click-worthy without changing who the game is for.

## What The Player Is Trying To Uncover, Protect, Collect, Or Avoid

The player is trying to protect their own battery, collect dropped charge cells, and avoid getting pinned into the rails or drained out by rival cars and hazard props.

## Why The Player's Main Verb Feels Desirable Inside The Fantasy

Ramming rivals in a toy RC arena feels playful and mischievous rather than cruel. It reads like controlled tabletop chaos: out-driving, out-bumping, and stealing the charge before the other cars do.

## Why A Player Would Want This Instead Of The Best-known Game In The Same Lane

The strongest pitch is not “another racer.” It is a compact survival brawler with no lap overhead, no weapons clutter, and a clearer battery-steal loop. The run starts paying off immediately: one clean ram can refill your battery and open the next aggressive choice.

## The Brutally Honest One-line Description And Why It Does Not Collapse Into "Famous Game X With A Skin"

Brutally honest description: `a tabletop RC-car survival brawler where you ram rival cars to steal the charge that keeps your own car alive`.

That does not collapse into “famous game X with a skin” because the player-facing differentiator is visible in the first minute: no laps, no guns, no routing, and a battery economy that turns each hit into both offense and survival.

## Why The Pitch Creates Immediate Desire Instead Of Merely Sounding Coherent

The pitch promises a desirable action immediately: drive a toy car fast, slam other toy cars, and stay charged long enough to dominate the table. It sounds playable and satisfying before any extra lore or packaging shows up.

## What Promise The Title, Icon, Hero, And First 15 Seconds Will Imply

They should imply charged toy-car chaos: one standout player car, rival red cars closing in, blue battery shards, bright desk props, and a confident electric-burst ram in the opening seconds. The build should honestly fulfill that promise with real direct-control collisions and visible battery drops.

## Why The Concept Supports A Real Player Session Instead Of A One-clear Toy

The run is not solved after one successful bump. The arena keeps escalating, battery pressure keeps the player aggressive, and enemy density changes the shape of safe movement over time.

## What Keeps Players Engaged After The First Clear

There is no “clear” in the v1 loop. Replayability comes from score chase, improving survival time, cleaner combo chains from back-to-back rams, and adapting to later enemy pressure and hazard clutter.

## Why The Game Is Distinct From The Studio's Existing Catalog

The studio account already covers route-switching, card play, rhythm, fishing timing, chain-reaction puzzle, bubble-shooter clustering, and tower defense. `Overvolt` opens a new lane: landscape direct-control vehicle combat with a bright tabletop toy identity.

## If A Starter, Demo, Template, Or Remix Path Is Involved, What The Player Will See That Makes It Clearly Not That Source

There is no starter, demo, or remix gameplay source here beyond the raw PlayDrop TypeScript template scaffold. The first screenshot and first `15` seconds will be defined by the actual tabletop arena, toy-car models, battery-drop economy, and ram-heavy survival loop rather than by any inherited sample scene.

## Why This Concept Deserves To Exist

It clears the current studio white space, uses a 100% proven interaction grammar, has an immediate physical fantasy, and can plausibly look worth clicking in a raw build without needing a rescue from marketing art later.
